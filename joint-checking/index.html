Betterment Joint Checking

Designed joint checking account experiences for both native mobile and web

Overview

While we were working on launching checking, it was obvious that joint checking should also follow. This fit with our mission of making cash management easier, and especially true for couples starting out with their finances together.

We worked parralel with our checking launch to add the ability to create a joint checking account with joint features.

Outcome

We will be launching this soon after conducting beta testing. Many existing customers converted their existing checking accounts to joint checking or create a new one altogether. We also saw many new signups as this was a feature many people were waiting out on for.

So why joint checking?

With Checking, it made sense to also add Joint Checking:

  1. Many of our customers are at that point in their life where they’re getting married or already married and need a joint checking account
  2. Joint Checking would compliment joint investing well within Betterment
  3. Simply, it was also just the most highly requested feature since Checking launch

How we built a joint checking account

We wanted to make it easy for people to create a joint checking account with their partner.

We allowed customers to easily add someone to their existing checking account, which would mean they would not have to update their billing or need to close out their account, or create a new separate account to be paired with their existing individual account.

We built out an invite flow that would allow the primary to add the secondary when creating the account by sending the secondary an invite. This was much easier said than done, as depending on the customer state of the primary and secondary, many different states and edge cases needed to be account for. Examples include if the account is under review, if they already had a checking account or not, and if they already had a Betterment account or not.

The primary could fill out some info for the secondary to both find them if they already had an account or help set up part of their account if they didn’t.

The secondary would be notified when they get the invite and had 30 days to finish setting up their account, giving them the leisure of finishing whenever they had time.

Once account setup would be complete, both people would have equal, shared access to their checking account where they can manage their transactions and move money together.

So what’s next?

Not just limited to checking, but especially with joint checking, that means there is twice the action, which means twice the noise. Allowing for more custom controls over notifications so people can be on top of their transactions would be an ideal next step to explore, along with further simplifying the signup process.
